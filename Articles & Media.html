Featured Essay

Opinion: National Days dilemma

Vancouver Sun

A full opinion piece exploring nationalism, public symbolism, immigrant identity, and the meaning of political loyalty in multicultural Canada.

Read the Vancouver Sun source

Come Aug. 14 and 15, some in the Pakistani- and Indian-Canadian communities will fly flags and sing national anthems to celebrate the independence days of their respective former homelands, ironically the very countries they left voluntarily to enjoy the Canadian way of life.

The displaying of flags originated on battlefields to identify warring factions. Although national flags have come a long way from serving that purpose, they remain potent political symbols of nationalism. The national anthem, while evoking emotional sentiments, also sings the praises of a country. What fascinates and baffles, however, is that many immigrants were not brought to Canada under duress or against their will, but chose to leave their homelands and come here voluntarily.

In a multicultural Canada, cultural celebrations are one thing; public displays of political loyalty to former homelands are something else entirely.

Celebrating St. Patrick's Day, Vaisakhi, or Caribana are cultural and religious occasions, not political ones, and they do not evoke dual political loyalty in the same way. "Loyalty" is the key word here. Singing national anthems and saluting flags of former homelands can project images of split loyalty and divided patriotism. It may be less troubling when Canadians of Italian or Chilean origins wave those flags during the soccer World Cup, but the symbolism remains political when national days are publicly marked.

Fortunately, in today's multicultural Canada, one enjoys the liberty to speak in one's mother tongue, eat ethnic cuisine, dress in national attire, and participate in cultural events from coast to coast to coast. All that the adopted country expects in return is political loyalty to one flag and one national anthem, that of Canada. Emotional attachment may remain, but it need not become a public political display.

The problem of divided loyalties also has roots in Canada's British political class. Canada's two political tribes, English and French, imported their historical political and cultural rivalries into the new country with little regard for Indigenous peoples already here. As a result of English-French struggle, the victorious British designated the Union Jack as Canada's flag, despite French-Canadian sentiment, until the Maple Leaf was finally adopted in 1965.

Foreign diplomatic missions quite properly fly their own flags on official buildings and host national-day receptions on embassy premises or in hotels. Canadians and members of diaspora communities may appropriately attend those events. Yet it is different when Canadians themselves host or organize celebrations for the national days of former homelands. The article points to a 2013 event in Toronto where the Indian Consulate General "sponsored" a public Independence Day program for the diaspora.

In the 1990s, Ottawa's mayor chose to fly the flag on the national day of each country with diplomatic relations with Canada and invited local diaspora communities to mark those occasions. The effort was criticized as a vote-getting exercise and later backfired in September 2014, when Vietnamese-Canadians strongly protested the flying of the flag of Communist Vietnam, the very regime many had fled.

Some may ask, "What's in a flag?" Yet after the narrow defeat of Quebec's 1995 sovereignty referendum, then-Deputy Prime Minister and Heritage Minister Sheila Copps ordered all federal government buildings across Canada to fly the Maple Leaf as a visible symbol of federal presence.

Canada, still a relatively young country, continues building its own institutions. The argument here is that Canadians should contribute to that nation-building project rather than reinforce signals of split loyalty and patriotism. As at the Olympic Games, one competes under only one flag and sings only one national anthem. Accordingly, the only flag Canadians need to fly is the Maple Leaf, the only anthem they need to sing is "O Canada," and the only national day they need to celebrate as citizens is July 1.
